# Choosing the Right PVA for Slime Making

When it comes to crafting the perfect slime, we need to choose the right Polyvinyl Alcohol (PVA). For slime making, partially saponified PVA—typically with a degree of saponification between 80 mol% and 90 mol%—is the standard because it balances ease of dissolution with excellent film-forming capabilities.

To make the ideal slime, you need to consider "Degree of Saponification," "Degree of Polymerization," "Viscosity," and "Cloud Point."

## Degree of Saponification
The degree of saponification indicates the percentage of hydroxyl groups in the polymer. For slime, the "88" series (e.g., 44-88) is most common.

### Solubility advantage
Unlike fully saponified grades, partially saponified PVA is soluble in cold water, making it much easier to prepare at home.

### Be careful for lump
Because it dissolves readily, it can form large lumps if added too quickly to water. To avoid lumps, add the PVA powder slowly to water at $25^{\circ}C$ or lower while stirring. Once dispersed, heat the solution to $90^{\circ}C$ for 30 to 60 minutes to ensure it is completely dissolved.

## Degree of Polymerization
the degree of polymerization represents the length of the polymer chain, determined by how many vinyl acetate molecules were linked during production.

### Strength and Elasticity
The degree of polymerization influences the strength of the PVA film and the elasticity of your slime. A higher degree of polymerization generally results in a tougher, more "rubbery" slime that resists tearing during stretching.

## Viscosity
Viscosity determines the thickness of your PVA solution. It changes depending on the degree of polymerization, the degree of polymerization, concentration, and temperature.

- Degree of polymerization
  - This represents the length of the polymer chain. A higher degree of polymerization increases the viscosity of the aqueous solution, which enhances the structural strength of the slime.
- Degree of saponification
  - This is the percentage of acetate groups converted to hydroxyl groups. The degree of saponification affects viscosity stability; lower saponification levels help mitigate excessive thickening or gelling at low temperatures.
- Concentration
  - This refers to the amount of PVA dissolved in water (measured in mass percent). Viscosity increases exponentially as concentration rises, meaning even a small addition of PVA can make your slime much thicker.
- Temperature
  - Viscosity is highly temperature-dependent; as the solution cools (e.g., from $90^{\circ}C$ down to $10^{\circ}C$), the viscosity increases.

## Cloud Point
The cloud point is the temperature at which a PVA solution begins to separate into two phases and appears turbid.

### Phase Separation
Because low degree of saponification (e.g., 80mol%) grades are more hydrophobic, they have lower cloud points than fully saponified types.

If the temperature rises above the cloud point, the solution may separate, affecting the slime's clarity. To keep slime clear, store low degree of saponification grades' solutions at $30^{\circ}C$ or lower.
